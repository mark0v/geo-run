# Design: Geo Run Frontier Settlement Builder

Generated by office-hours-style product review on 2026-03-31

## Problem Statement

Many people want light, sustainable motivation to stay active, but ordinary
fitness products often fail to create emotional momentum. Step counters and
health dashboards feel informational rather than rewarding. Social challenge
apps depend on competition and public accountability, which do not fit everyone.

The opportunity is to create a mobile game where real-world activity becomes a
soft, persistent source of progress. Instead of framing movement as exercise
compliance, the product should frame movement as fuel for building something the
player cares about.

## Demand Evidence

There is currently no direct demand validation for this product.

What exists today is:

- a clear founder hypothesis that standard health and step-tracking loops become
  boring quickly for non-athlete users;
- landscape evidence that activity-to-progress products exist and can find an
  audience;
- a product insight that emotional continuity may matter more than raw metric
  tracking for this user segment.

What is missing:

- observed user behavior with target users;
- evidence that users want a settlement-builder specifically;
- proof that people will return for this loop beyond novelty.

## Status Quo

Current alternatives include:

- phone step counters and Apple Health / similar health dashboards;
- watch rings, badges, and streak systems;
- social challenge products such as Strava;
- fantasy walk and route-based products.

The founder's current diagnosis of the status quo:

- basic tracking is boring and fades quickly;
- social pressure works mainly for users already motivated by sport or group
  accountability;
- route and achievement products still may not sustain interest for users who do
  not care about challenges or simple badges.

The real competition is not just another app. It is the current user behavior of
"I stop checking after the novelty wears off."

## Target User & Narrowest Wedge

### Target user

The best current target user is:

A person with some past affinity for games who now has limited time, wants to
maintain health or lose weight, and does not respond strongly to traditional
fitness tracking. They want progress to feel tangible even on imperfect days.

This user may use a phone alone or a phone plus wearables, but the product must
still feel rewarding without requiring advanced devices.

### Narrowest wedge

The smallest credible first product is:

- one frontier outpost;
- one primary daily resource from steps;
- visible building and expansion choices;
- a soft feeling of "my town moved forward today."

The wedge is not combat, squad tactics, or a giant map. The wedge is emotional
continuity through settlement growth.

## Constraints

- The product must work well using steps alone.
- Additional activity types should deepen the system, not gate it.
- The loop must stay low-pressure and avoid punishing inconsistent users.
- MVP scope must stay small enough to validate the loop before building a broad
  cross-game platform.
- Visual production should avoid dependency on complex animated characters.

## Premises

1. The primary user need is not tracking accuracy; it is emotional motivation
   through visible, cumulative progress.
2. Settlement growth is a stronger MVP loop than tactics-lite combat for this
   concept.
3. The player should never feel that a missed day erased prior effort.
4. The first game must be complete and satisfying on top of step-derived
   resources alone.
5. Activity ingestion, normalization, and game economy should be separable in
   architecture, but the MVP should still be built around one concrete game
   loop.

## Approaches Considered

### Approach A: Cozy Town Builder

Summary:
A soft city-builder where steps turn into construction resources and the player
expands a small town over time.

Effort: M
Risk: Low

Pros:

- Very clear daily loop
- Easy to explain to users
- Strong fit for low-pressure wellness motivation

Cons:

- Can feel generic if the setting is too plain
- Risks becoming too passive without terrain or strategic choices
- May overlap with casual idle-builder expectations

### Approach B: Frontier Settlement Builder

Summary:
The player starts with a remote outpost on new land and gradually expands into
surrounding terrain. Different activity types can later help unlock harsh
terrain, vertical districts, water systems, and advanced expansion paths.

Effort: M
Risk: Medium

Pros:

- Stronger fantasy and narrative than a generic town builder
- Better fit for terrain-based resource meaning
- More distinct from simple farm or idle games

Cons:

- Requires slightly more world logic and visual framing
- Needs careful design to stay cozy rather than punitive
- Can drift into over-scoped worldbuilding if unmanaged

### Approach C: Modular Activity Core with Swappable Games

Summary:
Design a reusable activity-processing core intended to support multiple games
over time, with the settlement game as only the first surface layer.

Effort: L
Risk: High

Pros:

- Strong long-term technical leverage
- Makes future gameplay experiments easier
- Encourages clean separation of health data and game logic

Cons:

- Easy to over-engineer before proving a single fun loop
- Delays user-facing validation
- High architecture cost for an unvalidated product

## Recommended Approach

Choose Approach B: Frontier Settlement Builder.

It preserves the most valuable emotional insight from the original concept while
avoiding the weakest part of the prior design. It gives meaning to daily
progress, supports future expansion into terrain- and biome-driven mechanics,
and remains distinct from direct route-tracking competitors.

Approach C should influence architecture, but not roadmap priority. The product
must first prove that one game loop is fun and sticky.

## Open Questions

- What visual theme is strongest for retention: fantasy frontier, sci-fi colony,
  or grounded cozy settlement?
- How many build choices per day are enough before the game feels like work?
- What is the first terrain barrier players should learn to overcome?
- What features keep the game from feeling like a passive timer-based builder?
- When, if ever, should optional light expedition systems appear?

## Success Criteria

For the first validation phase, success should mean:

- users understand the core loop without tutorial overload;
- even low-activity days still feel emotionally productive;
- players return regularly to see progress and make build choices;
- the city visibly changes often enough to reward daily usage;
- the game remains satisfying when only step data is available.

For broader market validation later:

- target users describe the app as motivating rather than merely tracking;
- retention beats simple novelty-driven drop-off;
- players can explain the product as a game, not just a fitness wrapper.

## Distribution Plan

Primary channel:

- mobile app stores on iOS and Android.

Technical distribution assumptions:

- Apple Health / HealthKit integration on iOS;
- Health Connect on Android;
- internal activity normalization layer before game-specific conversion.

Delivery strategy:

- prototype and internal validation first;
- MVP with one settlement loop;
- later expansion into richer activity sources and more game surfaces.

## Dependencies

- product decision on visual setting;
- MVP economy design for steps and optional floors;
- technical design for health data ingestion and normalization;
- lightweight visual language for buildings, terrain, and ambient life;
- future user interviews or prototype tests to validate the loop.

## The Assignment

Design the smallest emotionally complete MVP:

Define the first seven days of player experience in detail, including what the
player earns, what they can build, what visibly changes, and what makes day 5
still feel worth opening.

## What I noticed about how you think

- You consistently moved away from ideas that sounded cool but did not survive
  contact with MVP reality.
- You care a lot about keeping the game motivating without turning it into a
  punishment system.
- You naturally look for reusable architecture, which is a strength, but it
  needs to stay subordinate to proving one concrete fun loop first.
- The strongest product insight you surfaced was continuity: players should not
  feel they are starting over after an imperfect day.
