# Pivot Brief

## Pivot summary

Geo Run is pivoting away from a tactics-lite expedition concept and toward an
activity-powered frontier settlement builder.

The previous direction centered on a squad, missions, and a base. During product
discussion, the core weakness became clear: the smallest useful version of the
game did not support the fantasy. A one-hero mission loop would feel thin, while
full squad tactics would blow up MVP scope.

The new direction keeps the strongest part of the idea: real-world activity
should produce steady, meaningful, low-pressure progress. It replaces tactical
combat with settlement growth, terrain expansion, and visible world-building.

## Why we are pivoting

### What was strong in the old concept

- Real activity as game fuel was still compelling.
- A base or home hub felt like a strong retention anchor.
- The product benefited from a game loop deeper than a simple step counter.

### What broke in the old concept

- `One hero + missions` did not create enough tactical depth to feel satisfying.
- `Full squad tactics` required too much content, balance, UI, and design work
  for an MVP.
- Combat distracted from the strongest emotional promise: progress that keeps
  accumulating even when the player is inconsistent.

### What the new concept fixes

- Settlement growth naturally supports small daily progress.
- The game remains rewarding even on low-activity days.
- Visual expansion is easier to show than tactical combat is to design well.
- The product moves further away from direct overlap with map-walking apps.

## New product thesis

Geo Run is a cozy frontier settlement builder where real-world movement powers
the growth of an outpost on a new land.

The player begins with a small foothold and gradually expands it into a larger,
more alive settlement. They are not trying to win battles. They are trying to
transform a frontier into a thriving place.

## Core player fantasy

- I turn everyday movement into visible progress.
- My settlement keeps growing over time instead of resetting me emotionally.
- Even a weak day can still contribute something.
- I am taming and shaping a new land.

## Core loop

1. The player accumulates activity in the real world.
2. The app imports that activity and converts it into in-game resources.
3. The player returns to their settlement and spends those resources.
4. Buildings appear, modules upgrade, and new land becomes accessible.
5. The settlement looks larger, busier, and more alive over time.

## Resource model

- `Steps` are the universal building resource.
- `Floors` support difficult terrain, vertical construction, cliffs, towers, or
  hillside districts.
- `Swimming` and other optional workout data can later support special biomes,
  water expansion, canals, or advanced projects.

Design rule: the game must be fully satisfying with steps alone. Additional
health data should deepen the simulation, not gate the main product.

## World model

The game should not rely on animated citizen characters in the MVP. Progress is
expressed through:

- new buildings and modules;
- improved silhouettes;
- small ambient animations like smoke, lights, mills, conveyors, or moving
  water;
- terrain clearing and district expansion;
- a visible shift from empty outpost to living settlement.

This keeps production scope manageable while preserving a strong sense of life.

## MVP recommendation

### MVP focus

Build a small but emotionally complete settlement loop:

- one outpost;
- one starting region;
- one main resource from steps;
- one secondary resource from floors;
- a handful of buildings;
- visible expansion into neighboring terrain;
- soft daily return loop.

### MVP player ritual

The evening ritual should be simple:

The player opens the app, sees what today's movement earned, and makes one or
two meaningful decisions about what to build or unlock next.

### MVP value promise

The player should feel:

"I may not have had a perfect day, but I still moved my town forward."

## Systems we should delay

These are good future candidates, but they should not be in MVP:

- tactical combat;
- squad management;
- disaster systems that destroy player progress;
- deep citizen simulation;
- luck-based acceleration systems;
- heavy research trees;
- large overworld maps.

## Event philosophy

The game should avoid punishment-first systems early on.

### Avoid in MVP

- building destruction;
- catastrophic setbacks;
- decay that erases earned progress;
- harsh penalties for skipped days.

### Better long-term direction

If the world needs variety, prefer soft world events:

- temporary environmental conditions;
- positive bonuses;
- alternate build opportunities;
- small tradeoffs without loss of core progress.

## Randomness philosophy

Randomness is acceptable only when it increases upside, not when it threatens
fairness.

Good future use:

- chance to speed up construction;
- chance to improve research efficiency;
- momentum-style bonuses for healthy return patterns.

Bad use:

- losing spent resources;
- failed construction rolls;
- random destruction of owned structures.

Even in later versions, the base result should always be guaranteed. Randomness
should be a sweetener, not the backbone of progression.

## Product positioning

This product should feel like:

- more game-like than a tracker;
- more comforting than a challenge app;
- more alive than a raw city builder;
- lower pressure than a streak-based habit app.

## Open questions

- What exact visual setting fits best: fantasy frontier, sci-fi colony, or
  grounded cozy settlement?
- How many daily decisions are enough before the game feels like work?
- What is the first truly meaningful terrain unlock?
- How much strategic choice is needed before building becomes too passive?

## Recommendation

Continue exploration from the frontier settlement direction.

It keeps the strongest emotional insight from the original concept, reduces MVP
risk, and gives the product a clearer daily loop than the tactics-lite version.
